**Lawman, Unforgiven, and the Moral Collapse of the Western Lawman**

I have come to think that **Lawman** (1971) is one of the most underrated American westerns of its period. It is a bleak, intelligent, cold-blooded film that sits in that important transition point between the older studio western and the harsher revisionist western of the 1970s. It lacks the mythic reputation of films like *The Wild Bunch*, *McCabe & Mrs. Miller*, or *Pat Garrett and Billy the Kid*, and it is nowhere near as canonised as the major Ford, Hawks, Leone, or Eastwood westerns. Yet in some ways it is more disciplined than several better-known revisionist westerns. It is tight, bitter, nasty, and thematically very clear.

A great deal of that force comes from Burt Lancaster’s performance as Jared Maddox. Maddox is not the traditional western hero in any comfortable sense. He is not a swaggering saviour, not a romantic frontier figure, and not even a conventionally charismatic lawman. He is a hard, relentless professional, almost frightening in how calm, methodical, and absolute he is. The film strips away the old romantic frontier nonsense and presents law enforcement as grim, procedural, and morally corrosive. Robert Ryan, as so often, brings a worn-out ambiguity that deepens the film’s sense of exhaustion. The town itself feels morally spent. Nobody really wants justice; they want convenience, compromise, and the easiest possible path back to comfort.

What makes *Lawman* so strong is that Maddox is never treated as a simple hero. He is lawful, but the film keeps asking whether law without mercy becomes just another form of violence. That is what gives the film its bite. The title matters enormously here. The film is not called *Marshal* or *Sheriff* or *Gunfighter*. It is called **Lawman**, and that title is almost conceptual. Maddox is not merely a man who serves the law; he is a man almost reduced to that function. He is less an individual than an embodiment of law pushed toward monomania. The entire premise of the film, as the title suggests, is the moral ambiguity of the law taken to its extreme.

That, to me, is the film’s central insight. In a simpler western, law is assumed to be good because it stands against chaos. *Lawman* asks something far more unsettling: what happens when law is pursued without proportion, mercy, flexibility, or human context? Maddox is not corrupt in any cheap or obvious way. He is not a bribe-taker, not a hypocrite, not a cynical opportunist. He is worse in a more interesting way. He is sincere. He believes utterly in what he is doing. He is terrifying because he is not breaking the law; he is fulfilling it too completely. The law in him has become abstract, absolute, and dehumanised.

That is why I have always felt there are deep links between *Lawman* and **Unforgiven**. Not because the two films have the same plot, and not because one can be reduced to the other, but because they occupy related moral territory. *Lawman* feels to me less like a direct influence that can be proved than an earlier moral template that *Unforgiven* seems to echo. The resemblance is not in plot mechanics but in moral architecture.

The deepest resemblance is that in both films, law is not innocence. In older western mythology, the sheriff or lawman may be flawed, but the badge still more or less tells the audience where the moral centre lies. In *Lawman*, that certainty has already broken down. Maddox believes in law so absolutely that he becomes frightening. In *Unforgiven*, Little Bill Daggett occupies what should traditionally be the space of order and legitimacy, yet he is cruel, coercive, petty, and casually violent. In both films, authority and moral legitimacy are no longer the same thing. Both understand that the man who enforces violence in the name of order may be only a more socially acceptable kind of killer.

Maddox and Little Bill are not identical characters, but they rhyme. Maddox is cold, rigid, and self-disciplined. Little Bill is more mocking, bullying, insecure, and performative. But both are men who claim the right to define order, both use violence under cover of legitimacy, and both make everyone around them morally smaller. Both force the same disturbing question: when does the lawman stop being the barrier against violence and become its most official instrument?

There are other parallels as well. One of the clearest is between **Crowe Wheelwright** in *Lawman* and the **Schofield Kid** in *Unforgiven*. This is not just a superficial resemblance between two young men with guns. In both films, the younger gunman exists to expose the lie of western masculine glamour. Crowe Wheelwright is part of that emerging revisionist idea that the young gunfighter is not a noble rising hero but a reckless, reputation-hungry product of a violent culture. He is ambition without moral depth. The Schofield Kid is a fuller and even more devastating version of the same critique. He arrives full of swagger and invented legend, only for the fantasy to collapse the moment he actually kills a man. In both cases, youthful bravado is measured against older men who already know what violence really is. Both films treat the desire for a violent reputation as morally shabby rather than heroic.

The towns in both films also feel morally related. In *Lawman*, the town wants compromise, evasion, convenience. Nobody wants the full burden of justice if it threatens local peace or economic comfort. In *Unforgiven*, Big Whiskey is equally compromised. Its structures are based on intimidation, selective justice, male solidarity, and hypocrisy. In neither film is the community a stable moral centre. That matters because it removes one of the last consolations of the classical western. There is no clean town waiting to be saved.

Even the presence of the western whorehouse in both films feels meaningful to me. In *Lawman*, the prostitute world remains more peripheral, but it still contributes to the town’s moral atmosphere. It is part of the same worn-out social fabric of compromise, arrangement, and evasion. In *Unforgiven*, the brothel becomes far more central, but the underlying function is related: the treatment of prostitutes reveals the hypocrisy of male order. In both films, the edge of town tells the truth about the centre. The western sex economy is not just background colour; it is morally diagnostic. It exposes what the town’s law and honour are really worth.

Violence, in both films, is also treated in a way that strips the genre of glamour. *Lawman* is dry and unromantic. It is not interested in the catharsis of the gunfight. Its violence feels procedural, mean, and exhausting. *Unforgiven* pushes this critique even further, making killing ugly, humiliating, traumatic, and morally irreversible. Neither film believes in the old western fantasy that violence restores moral clarity. That may be the strongest bond between them.

For that reason, I see *Lawman* as part of the long road that leads to *Unforgiven*. It is not as rich or comprehensive, and it does not perform the same large-scale autopsy on the western myth. *Unforgiven* is broader, deeper, and more self-conscious. It attacks not just the sheriff or the gunfighter, but the whole mythology of courage, masculinity, reputation, heroism, and redemption. *Lawman* is still partly working within the lawman western structure, even as it darkens and corrodes it. But it is already presenting a world in which legality, masculinity, authority, and violence are morally entangled beyond repair. That is why the two films speak to each other.

This is also why I find the claim that *Lawman* is a remake of **Man with the Gun** so unconvincing. At best, that description is loose and misleading. Once a film is reduced to a thin synopsis like “a hard, uncompromising lawman enters a town and his rigid pursuit of order turns him into a disruptive, violent force,” one is no longer describing a specific film at all. One is describing a major western pattern. That basic structure can apply to *High Noon*, *Warlock*, *Hour of the Gun*, *Pat Garrett and Billy the Kid*, *Unforgiven*, and many others. At that level of abstraction, one is not identifying a remake relationship; one is identifying genre DNA.

That is precisely the problem with this kind of remake logic. It mistakes shared dramatic skeletons for direct lineage. The same bad method could be used across virtually every genre. One could call one noir a remake of another because both feature a cynical detective uncovering corruption. One could call one gangster film a remake of another because both show a ruthless outsider rising through violence and paying for it. That is not criticism; it is just vague pattern recognition elevated into a false claim of specificity.

A proper remake claim should require much more: close plot correspondence, recognisable character mapping, similar dramatic movement, and a strong sense that the later film is deliberately reworking the earlier one’s identity. *Lawman* does not meet that bar. In fact, the comparison weakens even further when one remembers that Robert Mitchum’s Clint Tollinger in *Man with the Gun* is not even fundamentally the same kind of figure as Jared Maddox. Tollinger is a “town tamer,” effectively a gun-for-hire imported by weak civic authority and then deputised. Maddox, by contrast, is a real law officer from the start, pursuing men under formal authority. That is not a small difference. It changes the whole moral shape of the story. In *Lawman*, the disturbing point is that law itself becomes relentless and inhuman. In *Man with the Gun*, the issue is closer to the town outsourcing order to a specialist in violence. These are related western ideas, but they are not the same idea.

That is why I think *Lawman* is far more interesting as an early revisionist lawman western than as some supposed remake. Its real significance lies elsewhere: in the way it darkens the figure of the lawman, strips the town of moral innocence, and shows violence not as heroic destiny but as professional labour carried out under legal sanction. It belongs to that long western argument about the unstable line between order and coercion, justice and legality, civilisation and violence.

In the end, what stays with me most is that *Lawman* is not really asking whether the lawman can defeat evil. It is asking whether the lawman, in becoming pure law, ceases to be fully moral. That is a far more modern and revisionist question than the old western usually allowed. Maddox is not just a character. He is almost a thesis. And that is why the film remains so potent. It is not merely a tough western about a hard man doing a hard job. It is a film about the moral danger of legality when stripped of mercy, proportion, and humanity. That is what makes it feel ahead of its time, and that is what makes it such a powerful precursor to the later westerns that would take those same anxieties even further.
