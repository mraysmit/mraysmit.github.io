## 1. Introduction

After more than two dozen viewings over twenty years, I have come to think that *Lawman* (1971) is one of the most underrated American westerns of its period. It is a surprisingly bleak, intelligent, cold-blooded film that sits in that important transition point between the older studio western and the harsher revisionist western of the 1970s. It lacks the mythic reputation of films like *The Wild Bunch (Peckinpah 1969)*, *McCabe & Mrs. Miller (Altman 1971)*, or *Pat Garrett and Billy the Kid (Peckinpah 1973)*, and it is nowhere near as canonised as the major Ford, Hawks, Leone, or Eastwood westerns. Part of the reason for that is the director. Michael Winner is not a name that gets mentioned alongside Peckinpah, Altman, or Penn when the revisionist western is discussed, and with good reason — his career trajectory toward the *Death Wish* films and beyond does not suggest a serious auteur of the genre. Yet in some ways *Lawman* is more disciplined than several better-known revisionist westerns, and the fact that it achieves what it does despite rather than because of its director makes the achievement more interesting, not less. The moral force of the film lives in Wilson's screenplay and in the performances — above all Burt Lancaster's — rather than in any directorial vision. It is tight, bitter, nasty, and thematically very clear.

A great deal of that force comes from Lancaster's performance as Jared Maddox. Maddox is not the traditional western hero in any comfortable sense. He is not a swaggering saviour, nor a romantic frontier figure, not even a conventionally charismatic lawman. He is a hard, relentless professional, almost frightening in how calm, methodical, and absolute he is. The film strips away the old romantic frontier nonsense and presents law enforcement as grim, procedural, and morally corrosive. Robert Ryan, as so often, brings a worn-out ambiguity that deepens the film's sense of exhaustion. Even the town itself feels morally spent. Nobody really wants justice; they want convenience, compromise, and the easiest possible path back to comfort.

What is most striking, returning to the film again and again, is how completely it anticipates moral territory that the western would not fully map for another two decades. The questions it raises about law, violence, legitimacy, and masculine identity feel less like the concerns of a 1971 genre film than the concerns of a much later and more self-conscious cinema. It is a film that belongs to its moment and yet seems to be reaching well past it.

Clint Eastwood's *Unforgiven* (1992), widely and rightly regarded as the definitive revisionist western and one of the great American films of its decade, is what it seems to be reaching toward. That is why I have always felt there are deep links between the two films. Not because the two films have the same plot, and not because one can be reduced to the other, but because they occupy related moral territory. *Lawman* feels to me less like a direct influence that can be proved than an earlier moral template that *Unforgiven* seems to echo. The resemblance is not in plot mechanics but in moral architecture.

For that reason, I see *Lawman* as part of the long road that leads to *Unforgiven*. It is not as rich or comprehensive, and it does not perform the same large-scale autopsy on the western myth. *Unforgiven* is broader, deeper, and more self-conscious. It attacks not just the sheriff or the gunfighter, but the whole mythology of courage, masculinity, reputation, heroism, and redemption. *Lawman* is still partly working within the lawman western structure, even as it darkens and corrodes it. But it is already presenting a world in which legality, masculinity, authority, and violence are morally entangled beyond repair.

## 2. Lawman as Precursor, Not Remake

**The remake claim**

Some critics and reviewers at the time of its release described *Lawman* as a remake of *Man with the Gun* (1955), the Robert Mitchum western directed by Richard Wilson, on the basis that both films feature a hard, uncompromising lawman whose rigid pursuit of order turns him into a disruptive and ultimately destructive force in the town he enters. That claim is lazy and naive, and the reasons why are instructive about how to think about *Lawman*'s place in the western tradition more generally. At best, that description is loose and misleading. Once a film is reduced to a thin synopsis like "a hard, uncompromising lawman enters a town and his rigid pursuit of order turns him into a disruptive, violent force," we are no longer describing a specific film at all.

**Maddox and Tollinger — an inversion**

In fact, the comparison weakens even further when one remembers that Robert Mitchum's Clint Tollinger in *Man with the Gun* is not even fundamentally the same kind of figure as Jared Maddox. Tollinger is a "town tamer," effectively a gun-for-hire imported by weak civic authority and then deputised. The town wants him there. It has invited the violence in because it cannot manage order by itself. Maddox does precisely the opposite. He arrives uninvited, against the town's wishes, pursuing men the town would very much prefer he left alone. The community in *Lawman* does not want what Maddox is selling, which is precisely what makes his pursuit so disturbing — he is not restoring order at the town's request but imposing law in the face of the town's active resistance. Maddox is a real law officer from the start, pursuing men under formal authority that the town cannot legally refuse. That is not a small difference. It changes the whole moral shape of the story. In *Lawman*, the disturbing point is that law itself becomes relentless and inhuman.

*Lawman* is far more interesting as an early revisionist lawman western than as some supposed remake. Its real significance lies in the way it darkens the figure of the lawman, strips the town of moral innocence, and shows violence not as heroic destiny but as professional labour carried out under legal sanction. It belongs to that long western argument about the unstable line between order and coercion, justice and legality, civilisation and violence.

**Gerald Wilson**

That argument gains an extra dimension when you look at who wrote these two films and when. Gerald Wilson, who wrote *Lawman*, is a considerably more interesting figure than his obscurity might suggest. He specialised in unsentimental westerns and thrillers that upended conventional takes on masculinity, race, and the nation's manifest destiny — a writer for whom virtuous heroes, redemption arcs, and happy endings were simply not available as options. He was not a craftsman who stumbled into moral seriousness; he was a writer who sought it out deliberately. His own description of *Lawman*'s design is precise: "There are no villains in the film, purposely. I tried to use every icon of the Western without the villain." That is a conscious artistic statement, and it explains a great deal about why the film feels so different from its contemporaries. Without a villain to anchor the moral structure, the genre's usual consolations — the righteous kill, the restored order, the vindicated hero — simply have nowhere to land. Winner, to his credit, was faithful to what Wilson gave him: "He did not change a comma. Not a comma! He directed what I wrote," Wilson has said. The moral force of *Lawman* is therefore unambiguously Wilson's, whatever one thinks of the direction.

The typically Wilsonian hero, as one writer has noted, is "an ageing professional with nothing more to learn about his craft" — a figure for whom competence has replaced everything else, including moral comfort. That description fits Maddox precisely, and it also fits the broader pattern of Wilson's work: *Chato's Land*, *Scorpio*, *The Stone Killer* — films that share a preoccupation with professional violence, institutional failure, and the absence of clean outcomes. There is a consistency to Wilson's vision across these films that makes *Lawman* look less like a fortunate accident and more like the fullest expression of a coherent and genuinely dark worldview.

**The Death Wish irony**

Which makes the *Death Wish* connection all the more striking. *Lawman* is a film that withholds exactly what popular cinema of the period was increasingly expected to deliver — the satisfying kill, the vindicated citizen, violence as emotional release. Wilson was in fact offered *Death Wish* (1974) before Wendell Mayes, who eventually received the screenplay credit. He was initially interested — the Brian Garfield novel on which it was based carried precisely the cautionary message that vigilantism was a problem rather than a solution, tracing the slow disintegration of a liberal into a killer who is out of his skull and out of control. That was material Wilson could work with. But when he was told the film needed Kersey to be a hero — that the villains would simply be made very bad to justify his violence — Wilson walked away. The film that was eventually made did exactly what Wilson refused to do: it romanticised the vigilante, delivered the catharsis, and became a phenomenon, condemned by many critics as an immoral encouragement of vigilantism but enormously successful precisely because it gave audiences what *Lawman* had pointedly refused to give them. *Death Wish* was deeply controversial on release, with Vincent Canby of the New York Times among the most outspoken critical voices against it. And yet it tapped directly into the urban anxieties of mid-1970s America and made Winner's career in a way that *Lawman* never could.

That Wilson walked away from *Death Wish* rather than writing it on the studio's terms is the more honourable and more revealing version of the story. It confirms that his refusal of the genre's consolations in *Lawman* was not accidental but principled — a consistent position that he maintained even when the money and the moment were pointing the other way. What survived on screen in *Death Wish* is the near-opposite of everything *Lawman* stood for: violence romanticised, consequences minimised, catharsis delivered without complication. The connection between the two films is therefore not one of authorship but of opposition. *Lawman* is the film that *Death Wish* refused to be.

There is one further connection worth noting, and it will be developed more fully in the discussion of revenge and motivation that follows. William Munny in *Unforgiven* is, at the level of dramatic structure, precisely the figure that *Death Wish* romanticises: a private citizen who takes lethal action outside the law in response to an official failure. The surface similarity is real. What *Unforgiven* does that *Death Wish* refuses to do is everything else — withhold catharsis, contaminate the motivation, show the human cost, and refuse to vindicate the avenger. Where *Death Wish* invites identification, *Unforgiven* produces dread. And where Wilson walked away from *Death Wish* to preserve the integrity of what *Lawman* had established, Peoples wrote *Unforgiven* to take that same moral territory to its fullest and most devastating conclusion.

**David Webb Peoples**

David Webb Peoples, who wrote *Unforgiven*, is a very different kind of writer — a writer of ideas who worked across genres, most famously co-writing *Blade Runner*, and who brought to the western a frame of reference that was explicitly non-generic. He has cited *Taxi Driver* as his primary influence when writing the script, which tells you a great deal. His interest was not in reworking western conventions from within but in applying to the western the same unflinching attention to how violence actually registers in human terms that Scorsese had applied to urban crime. The result was a script that dismantles the western from the outside rather than darkening it from within, which is precisely what gives *Unforgiven* its particular quality of moral devastation.

**Two writers, one cultural moment**

But the timing detail is the one that matters most for understanding the relationship between the two films. Peoples wrote the *Unforgiven* script — then titled *The Cut-Whore Killings* — in 1976. That is five years after *Lawman*. He was writing in the same broad cultural moment, responding to the same collapse of faith in the classical western's moral certainties that had produced *Lawman*, *McCabe & Mrs. Miller*, *Pat Garrett and Billy the Kid*, and *The Wild Bunch* in the years just before. The idea that Peoples was unaware of *Lawman* — a film with Lancaster, Ryan, Cobb, and a young Robert Duvall, released to reasonable critical attention in 1971 — is difficult to sustain. He was a cinephile writing speculatively with no director attached, in a period when serious writers reached for films rather than market trends. *Lawman* is exactly the kind of film that lodges in a writer's memory: cold, morally uncomfortable, unresolved, and doing something the genre had not quite done before.

What can be said with confidence is that both scripts were produced by the same historical pressure — the sense, in the early-to-mid 1970s, that the western's old moral vocabulary had become not just inadequate but dishonest. Wilson arrived at that conclusion through genre discipline. Peoples arrived at it through a consciously revisionist moral framework imported from outside the genre. One worked from within the tradition and found its limits. The other worked from outside and exposed its foundations. Between them, across two decades of production delay, they produced two of the most morally serious westerns in the American canon. That convergence is not coincidence. It is the product of a shared cultural moment working through two very different temperaments and two very different kinds of craft.

That argument — and the specific moral territory it opens up — is what the rest of this essay is concerned with tracing, both within *Lawman* itself and across the deep structural kinship it shares with *Unforgiven*.

## 3. Law Without Innocence

**The title as a moral argument**

What makes *Lawman* so strong is that Maddox is never treated as a simple hero. He is lawful, but the film keeps asking whether law without mercy becomes just another form of violence. That is what gives the film its bite. The title matters enormously here. The film is not called *Marshal* or *Sheriff* or *Gunfighter*. It is called *Lawman*, and that title is almost conceptual. Maddox is not merely a man who serves the law; he is a man almost reduced to that function. He is less an individual than an embodiment of law pushed toward monomania. The entire premise of the film, as the title suggests, is the moral ambiguity of the law taken to its extreme.

That, to me, is the film's central insight. In a simpler western, law is assumed to be good because it stands against chaos. *Lawman* asks something far more unsettling: what happens when law is pursued without proportion, mercy, flexibility, or human context? Maddox is not corrupt in any cheap or obvious way. He is not a bribe-taker, not a hypocrite, not a cynical opportunist. He is worse in a more interesting way. He is sincere. He believes utterly in what he is doing. He is terrifying because he is not breaking the law; he is fulfilling it too completely. The law in him has become abstract, absolute, and dehumanised.

**Authority without legitimacy — Maddox and Little Bill**

The deepest resemblance is that in both films, law is not innocence. In older western mythology, the sheriff or lawman may be flawed, but the badge still more or less tells the audience where the moral centre lies. In *Lawman*, that certainty has already broken down. Maddox believes in law so absolutely that he becomes frightening. In *Unforgiven*, Little Bill Daggett occupies what should traditionally be the space of order and legitimacy, yet he is cruel, coercive, petty, and casually violent. In both films, authority and moral legitimacy are no longer the same thing. Both understand that the man who enforces violence in the name of order may be only a more socially acceptable kind of killer.

Maddox and Little Bill are not identical characters, but their parallels are clear. Maddox is cold, rigid, and self-disciplined. Little Bill is more mocking, bullying, insecure, and performative. But both are men who claim the right to define order, both use violence under cover of legitimacy, and both make everyone around them morally smaller. Both force the same disturbing question: when does the lawman stop being the barrier against violence and become its most official instrument?

In *Lawman*, Jared Maddox is the law, but he is also a relentless agent of violence. The badge does not purify what he does. It authorises it.

In *Unforgiven*, Eastwood goes even further: the sheriff, Little Bill, is the law, but he is brutal, vain, and coercive. The film destroys the old fantasy that lawful violence is morally superior to outlaw violence. Both films are interested in the ugly fact that enforcing order often looks a lot like domination.

In the end, what stays with me most is that *Lawman* is not really asking whether the lawman can defeat evil. It is asking whether the lawman, in becoming pure law, ceases to be fully moral. That is a far more modern and revisionist question than the old western usually allowed. Maddox is not just a character. He is almost a thesis. And that is why the film remains so potent. It is not merely a tough western about a hard man doing a hard job. It is a film about the moral danger of legality when stripped of mercy, proportion, and humanity. That is what makes it feel ahead of its time, and that is what makes it such a powerful precursor to the later westerns that would take those same anxieties even further.

## 4. Proportionality, Process, and the Failure of Justice

Both films treat law not just as force, but as process — and it is in the treatment of process that their most disturbing ironies become visible.

**Lawman — when following the law destroys it**

In *Lawman*, Maddox is pursuing something that is formally legal and procedurally correct. He wants the men taken back to face court justice. But the bitter irony is that the original offence — however serious and tragic — was accidental, and in any functioning system it would likely have resulted in something limited, perhaps a fine or some lesser punishment rather than death. Yet Maddox's absolute insistence on legal process at any cost turns that relatively bounded offence into a trail of corpses. That is why the film becomes so disturbing. The law is not failing through weakness or corruption. It is becoming lethal through inflexibility. What begins as an attempt to deliver men to court ends by effectively condemning or executing them through the violence required to get them there.

That is one of the film's deepest ironies: Maddox is pursuing the judicial process, yet his pursuit makes due process almost irrelevant, because the path to court becomes deadlier than the court itself was ever likely to be. That is a very dark idea. It means the lawman's fidelity to the system destroys the very proportionality that should justify the system in the first place.

What is at stake in both *Lawman* and *Unforgiven* is not simply whether the law is present or absent. It is whether the law preserves the thing that morally justifies its existence in the first place: proportionality. A legal order is supposed to restrain violence, measure response, distinguish degrees of guilt, and prevent private passion or brute force from becoming the whole basis of justice. In both films, that promise breaks down. But it breaks down differently.

In *Lawman*, the dark irony is that Jared Maddox remains faithful to the legal system in form, yet destroys its moral meaning in practice. He wants the men brought back to face court. On paper, that sounds like the most civilised possible objective. He is not proposing lynching, revenge, or summary execution. He is insisting on due process. But the film gradually reveals the perverse result: his relentless pursuit produces far more violence than the likely legal outcome ever would have. The original shooting is accidental. However serious it is, it is not a cold-blooded murder in the ordinary sense. In a proportional system, the punishment would presumably reflect that. But Maddox's refusal to bend, pause, negotiate, or weigh consequence means that the route to justice becomes catastrophically disproportionate to the offence.

That is what makes the film so bleak. Maddox is not betraying the law. He is obeying it with such purity that he empties it of its human rationale. A legal system is supposed to replace escalating violence with measured judgement. Yet under Maddox, the attempt to preserve legal authority unleashes a sequence of deaths that effectively overwhelms the original crime. In that sense, his fidelity to the system destroys the very principle that makes the system worth defending. The law ceases to be a check on violence and becomes the mechanism through which violence expands.

That is why *Lawman* is more disturbing than a simple "harsh sheriff" story. Maddox is not just overzealous. He exposes a structural danger inside legality itself: that once duty is detached from mercy, context, or proportion, the moral superiority of law over vengeance starts to collapse. He still claims the language of process, but the process has become inseparable from destruction. It is almost as if the law has kept its external form while losing its ethical content.

**Unforgiven — when the law looks away**

In *Unforgiven*, the same idea appears, but from the other side. There the law does not become disproportionate through excessive fidelity. It becomes disproportionate through failure, trivialisation, and moral misrecognition. Little Bill does not pursue justice too far. He does not pursue it far enough even to count as justice. Delilah is horribly disfigured, and his response is to convert her suffering into a shabby local settlement. Compensation goes not to the victim but to Skinny, because his concern is less moral injury than damaged male property and local order. This is where proportionality breaks down in a different but equally damning way: the punishment does not match the crime because the law refuses to see the crime clearly in the first place.

That failure is not just procedural; it is moral. A legal order justifies itself by claiming to recognise harm in a more rational and humane way than revenge can. But Little Bill's judgement is so grossly inadequate that it destroys confidence in law as a vehicle of justice. Once that happens, private vengeance acquires legitimacy, at least emotionally. The prostitutes' bounty emerges precisely because the official structure has failed to preserve any believable proportion between injury and response. If *Lawman* shows the road to court becoming more violent than the eventual sentence, *Unforgiven* shows the legal sentence becoming so contemptibly slight that revenge steps in to fill the vacuum.

**Two ways law forfeits its claim**

So both films reveal the same deep problem: when proportionality collapses, law loses the moral distinction that separates it from violence. In *Lawman*, that collapse comes through excess. In *Unforgiven*, it comes through deficiency. Maddox overpreserves law until it becomes lethal. Little Bill underenforces justice until law becomes morally hollow. One produces too much violence in order to maintain legality; the other produces too little justice to sustain legality's legitimacy.

That is why the two films speak to each other so powerfully. Both are really asking what gives law its moral authority. The answer cannot simply be force, office, or procedure. It has to involve measured judgement, recognition of human harm, and some credible relation between offence and consequence. Once that balance is gone, the lawman no longer looks like the civilising alternative to violence. He becomes either its most relentless instrument or its most cynical manager.

You can even say that both films dismantle the classical western faith that legal authority is self-justifying. In older westerns, the badge often carries built-in moral weight. In these films, it does not. It has to earn that weight through proportion, and neither Maddox nor Little Bill can do that. Maddox makes legality so absolute that it becomes destructive. Little Bill makes legality so shabby that it becomes contemptible. Between them, they show two ways a legal order can forfeit the moral grounds of its own authority.

What both *Lawman* and *Unforgiven* finally expose is that law is only morally defensible when it preserves proportion. In *Lawman*, Maddox's fidelity to formal justice becomes so relentless that the pursuit of court judgement produces more death than the likely judgement itself, turning legality into a vehicle of excess. In *Unforgiven*, Little Bill fails in the opposite way, reducing Delilah's mutilation to a crude local settlement so inadequate that the law forfeits its claim to justice altogether. One film shows proportion destroyed by over-enforcement, the other by under-enforcement. In both cases, once law loses proportion, it loses the very principle that distinguishes it from violence.

## 5. Revenge, Justice, and the Moral Instability of Violence

The plot engine is one of the biggest differences between the two films, and that difference is itself revealing.

**Lawman's deliberately simple trigger**

In *Lawman*, the triggering incident is almost starkly simple: drunken cowboys accidentally kill an old man. That simplicity feels deliberate. The film strips the case down to basics so that the real issue is not mystery, emotional complexity, or competing narratives of victimhood. The issue is what happens when law pursues a plain case with absolute, inhuman consistency. In other words, the simplicity of the crime is not a weakness. It is part of the design. It clears away distractions so the film can focus on the moral problem of legality taken to its extreme.

**A corrupted world and a law that looks away**

In *Lawman*, the law is not initially discredited by obvious failure. On the contrary, Maddox arrives with a case that appears straightforward and legitimate. The law seems, at first, to be functioning. The moral corruption of Sabbath is visible in the town's evasion and self-interest, but it does not discredit the legal claim Maddox is pursuing. The problem emerges from within the law itself — because Maddox's pursuit becomes so rigid, so absolute, and so emptied of mercy that law itself starts to feel inhuman. The world around him is corrupt, but the law is not looking away. It is staring too hard and too mercilessly at everything in its path.

In *Unforgiven*, by contrast, the initiating wrong is morally messier from the start. The attack on Delilah is appalling, but the film immediately places it inside a larger network of prostitution, male power, humiliation, money, and compromised justice. Then the bounty introduced by the prostitutes further muddies the morality. What begins as a response to injustice quickly takes on the logic of revenge and contract killing, and attracts men whose reasons for coming have little to do with Delilah and everything to do with money, reputation, and violence. So where *Lawman* begins with a stripped-down legal offence, *Unforgiven* begins with a socially embedded act of violence that cannot be cleanly separated into justice and revenge.

In *Unforgiven*, there is no real ambiguity at the start about whether law has failed justice. It plainly has. Delilah is mutilated, and Little Bill's response is not justice in any serious moral sense. He treats the assault as if it were basically a matter of damaged property and disrupted economic order. The cowboys are made to compensate Skinny, not Delilah. That detail tells you everything. The law does not recognise her suffering as fully human suffering. It absorbs the crime into a male system of exchange, convenience, and authority. And crucially, this is not incompetence. Little Bill has the tools and the authority to deliver something closer to justice. He simply chooses not to. He chooses to recognise Skinny's property interest over Delilah's bodily integrity, without visible discomfort, because within his understanding of the order he maintains, that is simply the correct weighting. That is why the revenge plot in *Unforgiven* has so much force, even though the film finally refuses to romanticise it. The audience can immediately see why the prostitutes turn outside the law. They do so because the official structure has already exposed itself as morally bankrupt.

**A just cause, a dirty means**

It is worth pausing here on a connection flagged in the discussion of Gerald Wilson and *Death Wish*. Munny is, on the surface, the same dramatic figure as Paul Kersey in *Death Wish*: a private individual who rides out to kill men the law has declined to punish, in response to an official failure of justice. The bounty plot of *Unforgiven* inhabits the same structural territory as the vigilante film. *Unforgiven* is not interested in romanticising that territory. It is interested in exposing what it actually costs — in moral terms, in human terms, and in the integrity of the original grievance. That is the distinction that *Death Wish* never makes and that *Unforgiven* insists on at every turn.

That gives a very useful contrast. *Lawman* asks what happens when a simple case of justice is pursued so rigidly that justice starts to resemble cruelty. In *Lawman* there is no bounty, no hired killing, no private enterprise of revenge. The moral instability comes entirely from the law's own machinery. *Unforgiven* asks what happens when a genuine moral grievance is pursued through revenge, money, and killing, until the distinction between justice and vengeance becomes almost impossible to sustain. That difference matters because it shows that the two films are working on related terrain from opposite directions.

The bounty cannot be evaluated in isolation — held up against some abstract standard of justice and found wanting. It has to be measured against the only available comparison the film actually offers, which is what Little Bill did. And against that comparison, the bounty looks considerably less morally shabby than it might otherwise appear. The bounty is a response, not an initiation. Little Bill acted first. The extralegal violence that follows is downstream of a deliberate official choice. But it is nevertheless genuinely contaminated as a moral instrument. A bounty is a commercial transaction applied to human lives. It recruits men whose motives are at best mixed and at worst entirely mercenary, and whatever comes out the other end cannot be cleanly called justice even if it feels like it.

**Maddox and Munny: two kinds of impure motivation**

In *Lawman*, Maddox's motivation is impure in the most disturbing possible way. He is not mercenary. But he is not moral either, in any human sense. His pursuit is professional and almost impersonal — he is fulfilling a function, not serving a cause. The law has replaced motivation entirely. Munny in *Unforgiven* is impure in a different and more visible way. He is not riding out for Delilah. He barely knows she exists. He needs the money. His farm is failing, his children need provision, and the bounty is a solution to a practical problem as much as a moral cause. The film never lets him present himself as a crusader — the impurity of his motives is visible from the start. Between them, Maddox and Munny map two ways a man can act with lethal consequence while being entirely detached from the moral claim that supposedly justifies the action.

**The Schofield Kid and the death of the moral claim**

In *Lawman*, Crowe Wheelwright is drawn into the enterprise around Maddox by vanity and the pull of gunfighter culture rather than any real stake in the legal claim being pursued. His presence is revealing precisely because it shows that the world Maddox moves through is already populated by men for whom violence is glamorous rather than serious. But Crowe remains a secondary figure — he does not carry the moral argument the way the Schofield Kid does in *Unforgiven*.

The Schofield Kid's motives go further still in the wrong direction. He has entered the enterprise almost entirely through glamour and the desire for violent self-definition. His engagement with Delilah's suffering is essentially zero. He is there for the legend, for the reputation, for the chance to step into myth. The moral claim of the original grievance, already compromised by the time it reaches Munny, has by the time it reaches the Kid been reduced to almost nothing. A bounty recruits people with their own agendas, and those agendas progressively displace the moral logic that supposedly justified the whole enterprise. This is one of the ways *Unforgiven* shows that revenge, even when it originates in a legitimate grievance, tends to attract forces that have nothing to do with justice and everything to do with opportunity, fantasy, and violence for its own sake.

**The contrast: law's excess versus law's failure**

This is the genuinely hard question both films are circling, from opposite directions. In *Lawman*, the question is at what point law's excess itself becomes a form of injustice that licenses resistance. The townspeople and the rancher's men who turn against Maddox are not doing so for morally admirable reasons — theirs is the resistance of the powerful protecting privilege, not the powerless seeking justice. But the question of when relentless legal pursuit tips into something that demands to be resisted is one *Lawman* raises without quite answering. In *Unforgiven*, the equivalent question runs the other way: how inadequate does an official response have to be before extralegal action becomes not merely understandable but justified? If Little Bill had delivered a slightly lenient sentence, private revenge would look like impatience with due process. But he did not deliver a slightly lenient sentence. He delivered horses to Skinny and called it settled. The prostitutes are not naive about what they are doing. They have simply concluded that the world they are in leaves no cleaner option.

So the contrast is finally very sharp. In *Lawman*, justice fails because the law is pursued too completely, too mechanically, too mercilessly — and the moral instability comes not from a vacuum but from an overwhelming presence. In *Unforgiven*, justice fails because the law is inadequate, corrupt, and morally hollow from the outset, and the vacuum it leaves is filled by a revenge plot that carries a real moral claim but expresses it through means the film refuses to romanticise. One film shows what happens when law fills every available space until nothing else can breathe. The other shows what happens when law abandons the space it was supposed to occupy. And in both cases, the final violence settles nothing. Maddox's pursuit leaves behind a trail of deaths disproportionate to the original offence and a town that is not better but simply quieter. Munny's killings do not restore Delilah's face, return Ned, or give the prostitutes the recognition the law denied them. What remains in each case is the bitter knowledge that this is what justice looks like when it has been either consumed from within or abandoned from the start.

## 6. The Lawman as Official Killer

This is one of the strongest links between the two films, and it operates on the level of character rather than plot.

Maddox in *Lawman* is terrifying precisely because he is not hot-headed. He is calm, methodical, certain. He does his job and keeps doing it. William Munny in *Unforgiven* is similar in a different register. For most of the film he is tired, diminished, almost broken. But once he re-enters violence, the film reminds you that what makes him dangerous is not bravado but competence. In both films, the real menace is not theatrical evil. It is expertise.

Maddox is not a glamorous saviour. He is rigid, emotionally stunted, and increasingly isolated. The film's one sustained glimpse beneath that rigidity — his history with Laura Shelby, the woman who has remained in Sabbath and built a life there while he has gone on being the law elsewhere — is precisely what makes the stuntedness legible rather than merely asserted. Their scenes together carry an undertow of roads not taken, of a man who chose function over feeling so completely and so long ago that the choice is now irreversible. The film does not linger on this or sentimentalise it; it uses it to confirm that what Maddox has sacrificed is not just warmth but the full possibility of ordinary human life. That is what makes him frightening in a way that goes beyond professional severity. He is not a man who has suppressed his humanity in service of a mission. He is a man for whom the suppression has become the identity.

**Laura Shelby and Claudia**

The contrast with Munny's relationship to his dead wife Claudia is pointed and worth pressing. Where Maddox has a living woman he cannot return to, Munny has a dead one he cannot stop returning to. Laura Shelby exists in *Lawman* as evidence of a road definitively not taken — she is present, real, and unreachable because Maddox has made himself unreachable. Claudia in *Unforgiven* is absent in body but functions as a persistent moral presence. She is invoked constantly, by Munny himself, by his children, by the film's framing device, and what her memory represents is not simply grief but a standard. She is the person who reformed him, who saw something in him worth reforming, and whose death has removed the force that kept the worst of him in check. The cold logic of this is that Maddox is frozen by a choice he made while still alive to everything; Munny is haunted by a loss that has left him dangerously unmoored. Maddox sacrificed his emotional life in order to become the law. Munny's emotional life was briefly restored by a woman who is now gone, and the film's real dread is what fills that vacancy. One man walled himself off. The other had the wall built for him by bereavement. Between them they map the two ways a violent man can end up alone with his violence: by decision, and by devastation.

Munny is even more radical as an anti-hero: a former killer, a failed farmer, a man with a rotten past who is not redeemed by violence but dragged back into it. Both films strip the genre of romance. Men who kill are not admirable icons. They are damaged, feared, and morally compromised.

**Ned Logan's interrogation**

The brutal punishment of Ned Logan by Little Bill is particularly instructive when contrasted with the effective absence of punishment for the cowboys who mutilated Delilah. What makes that contrast so striking is that Little Bill proves he is not lenient, weak, or opposed to punishment as such. He is perfectly capable of brutality. In fact, he is very good at it. The issue is not that he dislikes violence. The issue is who he chooses to punish, and when.

The cowboys who mutilate Delilah receive what is effectively a negotiated commercial penalty. Little Bill minimises the crime, treats it as a disturbance to local order and property, and refuses to recognise the full moral seriousness of the attack. But when Ned Logan falls into his hands, Little Bill suddenly becomes the full coercive state: torture, intimidation, exemplary punishment, public display. That contrast reveals everything.

It shows that Little Bill's law is not guided by justice in any consistent sense. It is guided by control. Delilah's attackers are dealt with mildly because punishing them properly would require Little Bill to acknowledge a prostitute's suffering as morally serious. He will not do that. Ned, by contrast, is punished ferociously because he represents a direct challenge to Little Bill's authority. So the scale of punishment in the film has almost nothing to do with the scale of the original wrong. It has to do with whether Little Bill's personal order has been threatened.

**Indifference to justice, devotion to power**

That is why the contrast is so damning. The cowboys commit a vile act of violence against a vulnerable woman and are treated as a nuisance to be managed. Ned becomes part of a challenge to Little Bill's rule and is treated as an enemy to be broken. Punishment is not distributed according to moral injury. It is distributed according to political usefulness.

That is a huge revisionist point. The sheriff is not the neutral guardian of justice. He is the man who decides which violence counts, whose suffering matters, and what kind of force will reinforce his legitimacy. And this takes us back to the earlier point about proportionality. In *Unforgiven*, the law does not simply fail in the abstract. It fails selectively. It under-punishes the strong when the victim is socially disposable, and over-punishes the weak or marginal when authority itself is challenged. That is exactly what makes Little Bill's regime feel like a kangaroo court. It is not merely flawed law. It is law as an instrument of hierarchy.

Ned Logan is especially important here because he is also morally different from the cowboys. He is hesitant, worn down, unable to carry through the killing. He is one of the few men in the film who seems genuinely damaged by violence rather than exhilarated by it. Yet he receives the most savage punishment. That deepens the ugliness of it. The film is not just showing that Little Bill is cruel. It is showing that his cruelty falls hardest on someone already more human and more morally fragile than the men he let off lightly earlier.

So the contrast works with precision: the cowboys reveal Little Bill's indifference to justice; Ned's torture reveals Little Bill's devotion to power. Put together, they define his entire character.

It also creates another telling contrast with *Lawman*. Maddox is frightening because he applies law too uniformly and too absolutely. Little Bill is frightening because he applies punishment opportunistically and hypocritically. So one becomes monstrous through rigid consistency, the other through self-serving inconsistency.

## 7. The Young Gunman and the Collapse of Masculine Myth

There are other parallels as well. One of the clearest is between Crowe Wheelwright in *Lawman* and the Schofield Kid in *Unforgiven*, and it is worth beginning with Crowe, because he is where the revisionist move originates in this particular lineage.

**Crowe Wheelwright**

Crowe Wheelwright belongs to that emerging revisionist idea that the young gunfighter is not a noble rising hero but a reckless, reputation-hungry product of a violent culture. He is ambition without moral depth. He represents the younger man attracted to gunplay, posture, and status — someone who has assembled an idea of himself out of the violent mythology around him and not yet been made to pay for it. He is part of *Lawman*'s broader sense that the western world is populated not by noble citizens and clear villains, but by compromised men, poseurs, weaklings, and professionals of violence. In that way he helps strip away the glamour of the western male image not through a dramatic crisis but through his mere presence: the culture that produced Maddox also produces Crowe, and the two of them together — the absolute professional and the vain apprentice — between them expose how thoroughly violence has colonised every register of masculine identity in this world. But *Lawman* gives the role a deliberately limited weight. It is not really Crowe's film. The moral centre of gravity remains with Maddox and with the town's response to him. Crowe is a revealing secondary figure, not the main vessel through which the film stages the destruction of myth. He points toward a critique without fully embodying it. That is where *Unforgiven* goes further, and decisively so.

**The Schofield Kid**

The Schofield Kid is a fuller and even more devastating version of the same type. He arrives full of swagger and invented legend, only for the fantasy to collapse the moment he actually kills a man. Crowe is part of the same line; the Schofield Kid is the type brought to full tragic consciousness. In both cases, youthful bravado is measured against older men who already know what violence really is. Both films treat the desire for a violent reputation as morally shabby rather than heroic. But where Crowe remains a secondary figure whose role is diagnostic, the Kid becomes structurally central — one of the film's principal ways of dramatising what killing actually means.

The Schofield Kid is in one sense almost deliberately clichéd. He enters *Unforgiven* as a familiar western type: the young braggart, half-comic, half-pathetic, talking big, exaggerating his abilities, eager to attach himself to an older killer and step into legend. On paper, he can look like a stock revisionist device — the boy who still believes the myth that the film is going to destroy. But that would underrate how important he is. He is not just there to be exposed or mocked. He becomes one of the film's key moral anchors, precisely because he is the character through whom the western lie is most nakedly confronted and most decisively broken.

What matters is that the Schofield Kid still believes in the old language of gunfighter masculinity. He believes that killing is a route to identity, adulthood, reputation, and self-confirmation. He talks like somebody who has assembled himself out of western legend. He boasts, he performs, he compensates, he invents. He is full of fantasy long before he is full of experience. That makes him crucial, because *Unforgiven* is a film about the destruction of fantasy. Munny already knows too much. Little Bill knows too much in another way. Ned knows too much and wants out. English Bob has become pure performance. But the Kid is still at the stage where he believes the myth sincerely enough for the film to test it in real time. And when the test comes, he breaks.

**Moral anchor**

That is why he matters so much. Once he actually kills a man, the emotional and moral consequences are immediate. He does not become more confident, more heroic, or more fully initiated into manhood. He becomes horrified, shaken, and morally unsteady. The famous thing about that scene is not merely that the Kid regrets it. It is that the entire internal structure of the gunfighter myth collapses at once. He does not emerge as a man. He emerges as a child who has suddenly understood what men do. That gives him enormous weight in the film. He is the one character who most clearly demonstrates that the old western ideal of killing as proof of masculine worth is not just false, but psychologically ruinous.

He serves at least three major functions in the film's moral design. First, he is the carrier of myth: he brings into the film the old naïve belief in what a gunman is supposed to be. Second, he is the site of disillusionment: the film uses him to stage the collapse of that belief in the most immediate human terms. Third, he becomes a kind of moral witness: after the killing, he is one of the few characters who reacts in recognisably human terms — not with codes, poses, excuses, or systems, but with shock, guilt, and fear.

That is why "moral anchor" is the right description, but in a specific sense. He is not the film's ethical authority because he is wise or noble. He is the anchor because his breakdown restores moral reality to a world that keeps trying to bury it under language. He reminds the audience that a killing is not an anecdote, not a legend, not a reputation-builder, not a profession. It is the destruction of a human life, and it damages the killer too. In that sense, the Schofield Kid becomes the film's rebuttal to the whole mythology of youthful western masculinity.

There is another reason he matters. He makes Munny more legible. Munny is terrifying partly because he has gone beyond the Kid's stage. The Kid kills once and is shattered. Munny has killed before, many times, and carries a past so degraded that even he cannot look at it cleanly. The Kid therefore acts as a measuring device. He shows what a minimally healthy human response to killing looks like, which makes Munny's return to violence even more dreadful.

Eastwood and Webb are introducing the Kid as a figure the audience already recognises: the callow young would-be gunfighter, full of exaggeration, vanity, insecurity, and second-hand ideas of manhood. He is almost assembled out of western leftovers. That is not a flaw in the writing. It is the setup. The point is that *Unforgiven* needs at least one character who still arrives from inside the old mythology. Munny is already beyond it. Ned is tired of it. Little Bill manipulates it. English Bob performs it. But the Schofield Kid still half-believes it. He is carrying the cliché into the film so the film can break it in front of us. That is why his clichés are functional, not lazy. His boasting, his self-invention, even the slightly pathetic quality of his macho talk are all part of his role as someone trying to live up to a script he has inherited. He is not yet a man with a formed moral identity. He is a boy imitating legends. He talks as though violence will confirm him. And then the film makes him discover that violence does the opposite.

So although the Schofield Kid can seem at first like a somewhat conventional revisionist figure, he is actually structurally indispensable in a way that Crowe, for all his diagnostic value, never quite is. Without him, the film would still be dark, but it would lose one of its clearest demonstrations that the western myth is morally and emotionally fraudulent. Crowe shows the type; the Kid destroys it from the inside.

## 8. The Town as Witness, Accomplice, and Judge

The towns in both films also feel morally related, but the resemblance needs careful handling because the two communities fail in interestingly different ways — and that difference is sharpest when you look at what each town does when official law comes under pressure.

**Sabbath — the powerful resist**

In *Lawman*, Sabbath is not a town that has been abandoned by law or left without recourse. The problem is almost the reverse. Law has arrived, in the form of Maddox, with a claim that is formally legitimate and procedurally correct, and the town's response is not to rally behind it but to resist, subvert, and negotiate around it at every turn. The rancher Tetlow and the men around him represent a local power structure that has long operated with de facto impunity, and Maddox's insistence on legal accountability threatens the arrangement that has kept the peace on their terms. What follows is effectively a sustained attempt by the town's establishment to make the law go away — through bribery, threat, delay, and ultimately violence. Nobody in Sabbath is without knowledge of what has happened or without the means to act on it. The moral failure here is not helplessness but choice. The town closes ranks around its own interests and calls that arrangement order.

That gives *Lawman*'s vigilantism a deeply conservative and self-serving character. When the townspeople or the rancher's men move against Maddox, they are not acting outside the law because the law has failed them. They are acting outside the law because the law, for once, is refusing to be convenient. The violence directed at Maddox comes from those who have most benefited from the existing informal hierarchy, and who stand to lose most if it is disturbed. There is no moral insurgency in Sabbath. There is only the powerful protecting what they have by whatever means are necessary, including turning on the one man who is genuinely trying to hold them to account.

**Big Whiskey — the powerless push back**

In *Unforgiven*, the dynamic is almost exactly inverted, and that inversion is what gives the film's vigilantism its comparative moral weight. Big Whiskey is not resisting an overreach of law. It has been failed by law's deliberate underreach. The prostitutes who organise the bounty are not powerful people protecting privilege. They are among the most socially marginal and legally unprotected figures in the town, and they act outside the law precisely because the law has made it clear it will not act for them. Little Bill's settlement — the horses going to Skinny, the compensation bypassing Delilah entirely — is not a procedural error or a moment of weakness. It is a statement about whose suffering the law is prepared to recognise. Once that statement is made, the bounty is not a circumvention of justice. It is a response to justice's prior circumvention of them.

So the contrast between the two films on this point is stark and worth holding in view. In *Lawman*, the challenge to the law comes from above: from wealth, land, established local power, and a sense of entitlement to impunity. In *Unforgiven*, the challenge to the law comes from below: from those the law has written off, who have nothing left to work with except money scraped together and the hope that someone will come and do what the official structure refused to do. Both towns produce a confrontation between law and extralegal violence. But the moral character of that extralegal violence could hardly be more different. One is the powerful resisting accountability. The other is the powerless seeking it.

In neither film is the community a stable moral centre. That matters because it removes one of the last consolations of the classical western. There is no clean town waiting to be saved.

**Lawman, Unforgiven, High Plains Drifter**

"The town as moral witness or accomplice" is one of the great western subjects, and Eastwood pushes it to an almost terminal form in *High Plains Drifter*. In a classical western, the town is often weak, frightened, divided, or indecisive, but it usually retains some claim to moral innocence. It needs saving, or defending, or educating. Even when cowardly, it is still often treated as the community whose survival gives the hero's violence meaning. In these darker westerns, that changes. The town is no longer merely passive. It becomes morally implicated. It is not just a witness to violence, but an accomplice in the conditions that produce it.

That is certainly true in *Lawman*. The town is exhausted, evasive, compromised. It wants convenience rather than justice. It does not want to face the consequences of what has happened, but neither does it have clean hands. Its moral weakness helps create the space in which Maddox's severity becomes both possible and intolerable. The town is not innocent enough to judge him cleanly, yet not strong enough to resist him honourably. It is both witness and collaborator in the degraded order around it.

In *Unforgiven*, the same theme becomes sharper. Big Whiskey is not just a backdrop for Little Bill's rule; it is a place structured by intimidation, male solidarity, and selective moral blindness. The town absorbs Delilah's mutilation as a manageable disturbance. The men close ranks. The official order reflects the town's own values rather than standing above them. So again the community is not merely watching injustice happen. It is helping define what counts as injustice in the first place. That is why the town feels morally rotten before Munny ever arrives.

But *High Plains Drifter* takes this theme further than either of them. There the town is not simply weak, hypocritical, or compromised. It is collectively guilty. Eastwood almost strips away ordinary realism and turns the town into a haunted moral community. Lago is not just a place where bad things happened; it is a place built on denial, cowardice, and complicity. The townspeople are not only unwilling to act. They are implicated in the original crime. They watched, enabled, suppressed, and then tried to live on as if nothing fundamental had happened. That is why *High Plains Drifter* feels like an ultimate form of this idea. The stranger does not come to save the town in the classical sense. He comes to expose it, punish it, and force it to confront what it has tried to bury. The town is no longer the endangered moral centre. It is the object of judgement.

So across the three films you get an escalating pattern. In *Lawman*, the town is morally weak, evasive, and compromising. In *Unforgiven*, the town is hypocritical, selective, and structurally unjust. In *High Plains Drifter*, the town is guilty, haunted, and deserving of reckoning. That is a very powerful progression, and it maps a consistent Eastwoodian preoccupation with the moral failure not just of individuals but of communities — the places that choose to look away and then wonder why violence comes back.

## 9. Women, Prostitution, and the Moral Truth of the Town

Even the presence of the western whorehouse in both films feels meaningful. In *Lawman*, the prostitute world remains more peripheral, but it still contributes to the town's moral atmosphere. It is part of the same worn-out social fabric of compromise, arrangement, and evasion. In *Unforgiven*, the brothel becomes far more central, but the underlying function is related: the treatment of prostitutes reveals the hypocrisy of male order. In both films, the edge of town tells the truth about the centre. The western sex economy is not just background colour; it is morally diagnostic. It exposes what the town's law and honour are really worth.

Part of what makes that diagnostic function work in each film is that the brothel keeper is not merely a background figure. In both cases the keeper is a specific, active character whose relationship to the women under their roof tells you something precise about the kind of moral economy you are inside.

In *Lawman*, Vera is not a victim and not a grotesque. She is a pragmatist, shrewd and unsentimental, who has long understood exactly what kind of town Sabbath is and has built her operation accordingly. She is part of the same world as the ranchers and the local power brokers — she has simply been more honest with herself about what that world runs on. There is a bleak intelligence to her. She knows what Maddox is, what the town is, and what she is, and she makes no romantic claims about any of it. That clarity is almost its own form of integrity, and yet it is also the clearest possible expression of the film's broader point: that Sabbath is a place where accommodation has replaced principle so completely that even its most marginal figures have internalised the arrangement. Vera does not resist the moral atmosphere of the town. She embodies it with open eyes.

That is precisely what makes Vera so useful as a contrast with Strawberry Alice in *Unforgiven*. Both women occupy analogous positions — experienced, unsentimental, operating inside a world they did not design and cannot escape. But what they do with that position could not be more different. Vera's intelligence runs inward and downward, toward survival, self-knowledge, and the management of what is. Strawberry Alice's runs outward and upward, toward action, solidarity, and the refusal of what is. Vera has made her peace with the arrangement because she has concluded, correctly enough given Sabbath, that the arrangement is all there is. Strawberry Alice reaches the same conclusion about Big Whiskey's official order — that it will not serve her or Delilah or any of them — and draws the opposite lesson from it. Where Vera accommodates, Alice organises. Where Vera's lucidity leads to endurance, Alice's leads to insurgency. The bounty is her act, her conception, and in some sense her moral declaration: that if the law will not recognise Delilah's suffering as real, she will find another mechanism that does. She is not naive about what that mechanism is. She knows she is hiring killers. But she has made a judgement that the world she is in leaves no cleaner option, and she acts on it without flinching. Vera, confronted with the same kind of world, would have found a way to live with it. That is the distance between them, and it is also the distance between the two films' ultimate positions on what women at the moral edge of the western town are permitted, or compelled, to do.

What makes Alice so striking as a figure is not simply that she acts where Vera would not. It is the quality of her action. She does not organise the bounty out of sentiment, or grief, or romantic indignation. She does it with the same clear-eyed practicality that Vera brings to accommodation — the difference being that Alice's clarity has led her to a different conclusion about what the situation requires. She has assessed the official response, found it not merely inadequate but contemptible, and decided that if the law will not treat Delilah's suffering as real then she will find a mechanism that does. The bounty is not an emotional outburst. It is a considered judgement, made by a woman who knows exactly what she is setting in motion. That is what gives her moral authority in the film even as the instrument she chooses is compromised. She is not naive about the men she is hiring or the violence she is purchasing. She simply has no better option and acts anyway. In a film full of men rationalising, posturing, and retreating from moral clarity, Alice is one of the few characters who looks at what the situation actually is and responds to it directly.

But the more important figure in the brothel world of *Lawman* is Lucas, and he matters for a different reason entirely. Lucas knows Maddox. That prior acquaintance is what elevates the brothel scenes beyond moral atmosphere and into something dramatically central. He is not merely a keeper managing an operation in a compromised town; he is a man with a specific, personal understanding of what Maddox is and what his arrival in Sabbath means. Where everyone else in the town is reading Maddox from the outside — trying to calculate him, bribe him, threaten him, or outlast him — Lucas reads him from the inside, from accumulated knowledge of the man rather than the lawman. That gives their scenes together a register that is largely absent from the rest of the film: not the cold procedural confrontation between law and resistance, but something closer to recognition, and the exhaustion that comes with it. Lucas is not innocent, and the film does not pretend he is. He is part of the same worn-out arrangement as everyone else. But he also has a window into Maddox that nobody else in Sabbath possesses, and that window opens onto the film's deepest question — what it has cost Maddox to become what he is — in a way that no other character can quite manage. He functions, in that sense, as the film's most privately positioned witness to the human wreckage that legality taken to its extreme leaves behind.

Skinny in *Unforgiven* occupies a structurally similar position but with a very different and considerably uglier moral profile. Where Vera operates within the existing arrangement and has at least the honesty of someone who knows what she is part of, Skinny is an active participant in the conversion of Delilah's suffering into a property transaction. When Little Bill's settlement compensates Skinny rather than Delilah, this does not happen over Skinny's objection. He accepts and expects it, because within his understanding of the arrangement Delilah is damaged goods and the horses are the proper unit of reckoning. His concern throughout is commercial: the attack has disrupted his business and reduced the value of an asset. That he never frames it in those terms explicitly makes it worse rather than better. He does not need to. The logic is so embedded in the local order that it requires no articulation.

The contrast between these figures and Skinny is sharp and instructive. Vera is a pragmatist working within a corrupt system and knowing it. Lucas is a man whose personal knowledge of Maddox gives him a peculiar, privately held moral clarity about what the law costs those who embody it absolutely. Skinny, by contrast, is a man who has so thoroughly absorbed the dehumanising logic of his own system that he cannot see Delilah's injury as anything other than a disruption to his inventory. All three figures tell you what their respective worlds are built on. But where Vera's clear-eyed cynicism at least acknowledges the reality of what surrounds her, and Lucas's prior knowledge of Maddox gives him something approaching genuine moral witness, Skinny's obtuseness is of a more insidious kind. He is not cynical about the arrangement. He believes in it. And that belief — shared, apparently, by Little Bill — is what makes *Unforgiven*'s indictment of its town so much more comprehensive than *Lawman*'s. Sabbath is corrupt and knows it. Big Whiskey is corrupt and calls it order.

The whorehouse and the prostitute world in both films help define the moral temperature of the town. They sit at the edge of the main action, but they tell you a great deal about what kind of society you are in. The key point is this: in both films, the treatment of prostitutes reveals the hypocrisy of male order.

In a classical western, the saloon girl or prostitute is often just background texture, atmosphere, or a sentimental secondary figure. In these darker westerns, that setting becomes morally diagnostic. In *Lawman*, the whorehouse is more peripheral, but it still matters because it helps show the town as a place of compromise, accommodation, and moral fatigue. It belongs to the same social fabric as the ranchers, businessmen, and local power brokers. It is part of the world Maddox enters and judges. What matters is not that the film is "about" prostitution. It is that the town's social order is already half-corrupt, half-pragmatic, and entirely used to looking away from inconvenient moral truths. The whorehouse fits that atmosphere perfectly. It reinforces the idea that this town is not built on principle. It is built on arrangement.

In *Unforgiven*, the prostitutes are not just part of the background; they are the moral trigger of the plot. The mutilation of Delilah and the failure of official justice expose the whole town immediately. Little Bill's response tells you exactly what his law amounts to: not justice, but crude social management. So the brothel in *Unforgiven* is where the film's critique becomes explicit. Male violence is normalised. Women are treated as damaged property. The law protects order, not justice. Money and masculine hierarchy matter more than human injury. That is one of the reasons *Unforgiven* is so devastating. The prostitutes see more clearly than most of the men what kind of world this is.

In both films, the western sex economy sits near the edge of respectable society, but it actually helps expose the centre. The brothel is not just local colour. It is a place where the myths of civilisation and masculine honour start to fall apart. Both films are saying, in different degrees: look at how this town treats its most vulnerable women, and you will understand what its law and morality are really worth.

## 10. The End of Heroic Violence

Violence, in both films, is also treated in a way that strips the genre of glamour. *Lawman* is dry and unromantic. It is not interested in the catharsis of the gunfight. Its violence feels procedural, mean, and exhausting. *Unforgiven* pushes this critique even further, making killing ugly, humiliating, traumatic, and morally irreversible. Neither film believes in the old western fantasy that violence restores moral clarity. That may be the strongest bond between them. But the two films earn that shared position through very different means, and those differences are worth pressing.

**The aesthetics of unglamorous killing**

*Lawman* achieves its effect largely through register and pacing. The violence is dry, abrupt, and procedural. Victims do not fall beautifully. There is no musical swelling, no slow motion, no moment of heroic stillness before the draw. It feels like a job being done badly on both sides — ugly, sudden, and without aftermath. That flatness is itself a moral argument. The film refuses the genre's standard invitation to find violence aesthetically satisfying, and in doing so it refuses the emotional transaction that satisfaction depends on: the idea that the right man winning a gunfight means something beyond the physical fact of the right man winning. *Lawman* is dry, bleak, and increasingly fatalistic. Violence does not resolve moral tension; it intensifies it. Every death in the film feels like a subtraction rather than a resolution. There is no cleansing gunfight, no moment where blood restores order. Maddox moves through the town and its outskirts with the mechanical patience of a man processing an invoice, and the horror lies precisely in that quality: the aesthetic register of the film refuses to elevate what he does into anything resembling heroic ritual.

*Unforgiven* uses different tools to reach the same refusal. Where *Lawman* works through austerity and procedural coldness, *Unforgiven* slows down and makes you sit with consequence. The outhouse killing is the clearest example. The man inside is not a monster. He is frightened, begging, humiliated, dying badly in an undignified place. The film holds on that long enough to make the audience feel the wrongness of it even as it understands the logic that led there. It is the opposite of the clean western kill. And it is devastating precisely because it comes after so much of the film has been spent establishing, through the Schofield Kid and through Munny's fever and through Ned's death, that killing is not romantic, not redemptive, and not free. The famous line about killing a man — taking all he's got and all he's ever gonna have — is basically the thesis of the film. It is spoken in the aftermath of violence, not before it, which matters enormously. It is not a warning or a code. It is a reckoning.

**The asymmetry of competence and morality**

In both films the most dangerous man in the room is also the most morally troubling. The genre had long allowed competence with a gun to imply a kind of moral fitness — the faster draw as a rough index of the righteous cause. Both films systematically destroy that equation. Maddox is lethal and also inhuman. Munny is lethal and also historically monstrous. Violence is never in the hands of the righteous. It is in the hands of the most efficient, and efficiency carries no moral credential whatsoever. That is a more unsettling idea than simple villainy, because it cannot be resolved by defeating the villain. The problem is not who holds the gun. The problem is what the gun means and what it costs, regardless of who holds it.

**The cost to the killer**

*Lawman* is the colder of the two films about this cost, but no less insistent on it. Maddox does not break down, does not suffer visible crisis, does not confess or weep. But his increasing isolation is the equivalent register. The violence does not ennoble him. It hollows him further, drives away what little remains of ordinary human connection — and here the Laura Shelby thread runs deepest, because she is the one figure who might have represented an alternative, and the film quietly confirms that the alternative closed off long ago. His cost is paid not in dramatic crisis but in progressive subtraction. By the time the film ends, what is left is almost purely function.

*Unforgiven* is more explicit about the same cost, and externalises it in ways *Lawman* refuses to. Munny's fever and delirium early in the film are not incidental illness. His past surfaces as physical breakdown — the body registering what the mind is trying to suppress, the history of violence returning as something close to possession. And the Schofield Kid's collapse after his killing is the most naked statement in the film of what violence actually does to the person who commits it. He does not emerge hardened or confirmed. He emerges diminished and frightened, and his famous line about having thought it would be different is one of the most quietly devastating admissions in the western genre.

**The deaths of the wrong people**

In both films, violence has a way of falling on figures who carry a moral weight that makes their destruction the film's most damning statement. In *Lawman*, the machinery of legal pursuit grinds through people who are peripheral to the original offence — men whose involvement is tangential, whose guilt is limited, and whose deaths are the disproportionate product of Maddox's relentless forward motion. The law does not discriminate finely. It moves, and people in its path are destroyed by proximity as much as by culpability.

In *Unforgiven*, the equivalent figure is Ned Logan, and his fate is the film's single most important turning point. What makes Ned so crucial is the moment before his death — the moment at the outhouse when he finds he cannot shoot. That inability is not cowardice in any simple sense. It is the surfacing of a genuine moral interior, an instinctive refusal that the other men around him have either suppressed or never possessed. Ned already knows, in that moment of paralysis, what the Schofield Kid will only discover after pulling the trigger: that killing a man is irreversible, and that the self that does it is not the same self that existed before. He cannot proceed. He turns back. And that turning back — the most admirable impulse in the entire film — is the direct mechanical cause of his death. His moral scruple puts him on the road alone, and alone is where Little Bill finds him.

The film is devastatingly precise about this without underlining it. Ned is the most human figure in the group. He is hesitant, worn down, unable to carry through, genuinely damaged by the prospect of violence rather than exhilarated by it. And he receives the most savage punishment. Decency gets you killed. That is not a lesson the film teaches with any satisfaction. It is a fact the film presents with the cold clarity of a world that has no interest in rewarding the right instincts.

Ned's inability also recontextualises Munny in the most disturbing possible way. The fact that Munny can still shoot — efficiently, without hesitation once the fever breaks and the killing of Ned has given him back his rage — is thrown into sharp relief by what Ned could not do. Ned is what genuine moral reformation might actually look like: a man who, when the moment comes, finds that he cannot. Munny, by contrast, discovers that his reformation was always more conditional than he believed, always dependent on the absence of sufficient provocation. Ned's paralysis is the measure by which Munny's return to violence is understood as a return, not a revelation. And the contrast with Maddox deepens this further. Maddox never has a moment of paralysis. He never cannot shoot. That continuity of lethal competence is precisely what makes him frightening rather than tragic in the way Ned is tragic. Ned's failure reveals a moral interior. Maddox's consistency suggests the interior has long since been replaced by function.

**The refusal of the redemptive arc**

Classically, western violence resolves something. Order is restored, the hero is vindicated, the community is safer, the future is cleaner. Neither film allows any of that. The violence in *Lawman* leaves behind exhaustion, depletion, and a town that is not better for having been subjected to legal process but simply quieter. The violence in *Unforgiven* leaves behind a void — moral order destroyed, the most powerful man in the room revealed as the most morally terrifying one, and no version of safety or justice waiting on the other side. The full working out of this refusal belongs to the denouements, and is treated there in detail. But it is worth naming here as the structural consequence of everything else in this section: once violence is stripped of glamour, stripped of the equation between competence and righteousness, stripped of its claim to spare the good and punish the guilty, it can no longer perform the redemptive function the genre assigned it. What remains is just the thing itself — irreversible, costly, and morally inert.

---

## 11. Denouement, Emptiness, and the Refusal of Catharsis

The denouements are crucial, because they reveal the final shape of each film's moral argument.

*Lawman* ends with intention in its abruptness, and that abruptness is part of its force. There is no grand settling of accounts, no apocalyptic return of a legendary killer, no extended reckoning between opposed moral systems. Instead, the film reaches its end in a way that feels almost cut off, drained, anti-climactic in the best sense. That is not a weakness. It is consistent with the whole logic of the film.

Because *Lawman* is not building toward a mythic confrontation between criminal and law. It is building toward the exhaustion of law itself. The abrupt ending denies the audience any large cathartic release because the film has no interest in transforming Maddox into a legendary figure or his struggle into a final moral showdown. Instead, the film leaves behind the sour aftertaste of what his pursuit has cost. Its very abruptness suggests that there is no higher revelation coming, no redeemed order waiting on the other side, no grand western closure. The law has done its work, and what remains is depletion. *Lawman* gives that final emptiness directly: abrupt, drained, anti-climactic, with no mythic afterglow. It feels as though the law has consumed the moral meaning that was supposed to justify it, leaving behind only exhaustion.

*Unforgiven* ends very differently, and yet arrives at a related destination by a far more violent route. Its ending is operatic, almost apocalyptic. Munny comes back into town not as a reformer, not as an officer of justice, and not even really as an avenger in any clean sense, but as a man who has fully re-entered the identity the film has spent so long circling around: the killer. He confronts Little Bill, the sheriff, and in doing so the film stages one of its most brutal reversals: the criminal defeats the law. Or more precisely, the man outside the law destroys the man who claimed to embody it.

That matters because Little Bill is not defeated by a superior version of justice. He is defeated by an older, darker, more absolute violence. Munny does not restore moral order. He annihilates the existing order and exposes how thin it was all along. Once he enters the saloon, the distinction between lawful violence and outlaw violence collapses almost completely. The sheriff, the deputies, the town's armed men, the local structure of authority — all are shown to rest on force, and Munny is simply better at it.

That is why the line "killer of women and children" matters so much. It strips away any last protective romance around Munny. He is not just a wronged man taking revenge or a reluctant hero forced into action. He is a figure from the deepest moral darkness of the western imagination. The line drags his past into the room and refuses to let the audience convert him into a clean avenger. Even at the moment of Little Bill's defeat, the film denies us a morally satisfying triumph. Law is defeated, yes, but not by justice. It is defeated by a man whose own history places him beyond any comforting heroic category.

So the ending of *Unforgiven* is not simply about the criminal defeating the law. It is about the western finally admitting that the law and the criminal may be closer than the genre once wanted to believe, and that when the law fails badly enough, the vacuum is filled not by moral restoration but by older and uglier forms of power. Munny wins, but his victory is a catastrophe. It resolves the plot while poisoning any sense of moral resolution. Munny's return to violence is explosive, theatrical, unforgettable — one of the great final eruptions in the western — but what it leaves behind is not renewal, justice, or restored order. It leaves a void. The saloon massacre is so overwhelming precisely because it destroys any lingering illusion that the showdown will produce moral clarity. After the violence, there is only aftermath, fear, and the hard fact that the most powerful man in the room was also the most morally terrifying one. So *Unforgiven* also ends in emptiness, but it is an emptiness reached through violent revelation rather than through the cold anti-climax of *Lawman*.

That is the major structural contrast between the two endings. *Unforgiven* ends in a dark explosion, where violence tears through the town and reveals the collapse of lawful authority in spectacular form. *Lawman* ends in a kind of cold truncation, where the process of law has already hollowed everything out and there is almost nothing left to dramatise except the bitter fact of what it has become. In structural terms, *Unforgiven* gives you a final confrontation; *Lawman* gives you a final emptiness.

---

## 12. Myth, Legend, and the Biographer as False Witness

*Lawman* is a film without a myth-maker. Nobody in Sabbath is recording what happens, converting the trail of deaths into legend, or standing at the threshold between event and story. The film ends in cold truncation precisely because there is no narrativising apparatus at work within it. Wilson's stated principle — no villains, every icon of the western used but the genre's consolations refused — produces a world in which events resist being turned into story. What happens in Sabbath is not the stuff of legend. It is the stuff of exhaustion, and exhaustion does not narrate well.

That is why *Lawman* is anti-mythic not just in tone but in structure. It withholds the conditions that myth requires: the clear moral stakes, the vindicated hero, the restored order, the sense that what has happened means something beyond its own bitter mechanics. Maddox rides out and the town is left with its depletion. There is nobody standing at the threshold between what happened and the story that might be told about it. In that sense, *Lawman*'s bleak ending is also its most radical formal gesture — it denies the audience the narrative frame through which western violence normally acquires meaning.

*Unforgiven* does the opposite — not by romanticising its violence, but by including within itself the very figure whose function is to show how myth persists even after reality has exposed it as a lie. W. W. Beauchamp is one of the film's most important figures because he is the witness to legend at the very moment legend is being both created and destroyed. He arrives as a collector of western myth, a man who turns violence into language, reputation, anecdote, style. He wants the gunslinger as text. He wants the West in a form that can be narrated, packaged, admired. At first he attaches himself to English Bob, who is already a fraud of performance and self-invention. Then he shifts to Little Bill, who punctures Bob's myth and supplies his own version of authority. Finally he is present for Munny, who gives him the thing he has really been chasing all along: naked, undeniable, terrifying force.

That makes him the only real witness to the legend unfolding before his eyes, but the key point is that what he witnesses is not a noble legend in the old sense. He witnesses the birth of myth out of moral horror. That is why he is so important to the ending. He stands at the threshold between history and legend, between lived violence and the story that will survive it. And what the film suggests is that the legend will survive more easily than the truth.

The truth is ugly. Munny is not a redeemed hero. Little Bill is not civilisation. English Bob is not grandeur. Killing is not noble. The town is not morally restored. But Beauchamp is the sort of figure through whom ugliness becomes narrative. He is the mechanism by which a vanished or vanishing world is converted into story. And that is where the phrase is exactly right: he is documenting an almost — but not quite — vanished era. The West of classic myth is dying, perhaps already dead, but violence, domination, vanity, and storytelling are not dead at all. What is vanishing is not violence itself, but a particular world that could still pretend violence had grandeur, coherence, or heroic legitimacy. Beauchamp is there at the moment when that older idiom is collapsing, yet he is also one of the people who will preserve it in altered, misleading form.

So the biographer is not just comic relief or a satirical side figure. He is a profound figure of transition: witness, myth-maker, recorder, falsifier, mourner of a disappearing world, and agent of its survival as legend. That gives *Unforgiven* an extra layer that *Lawman* does not quite have in the same explicit form. *Lawman* is anti-mythic in its structure and tone, but *Unforgiven* actually includes within itself a character whose function is to show how myth persists even after reality has exposed it as a lie.

That may be the deepest link between the "final emptiness" idea and the biographer. Because in *Unforgiven*, the apocalypse does not prevent myth. It may even feed it. A man like Beauchamp can take the night of horror and turn it into the material of legend. So the film ends not only with emptiness, but with the unsettling knowledge that emptiness will later be narrated as meaning. The sharpest formulation may be this: *Lawman* ends by denying myth. *Unforgiven* ends by showing that even after myth is morally destroyed, someone will still write it down.

---

## Conclusion

*Lawman* is a great film. Bleak, disciplined, morally serious, and twenty years ahead of where the western would eventually arrive. It deserves to be watched and argued about. It has been ignored for long enough.
